Section 1. Statistical Test¶

Explore the diference between hourly entries when it is rain versa it is no rain. First, we test the null hypothesis that the distribution of hourly entries is Gaussian. We use D’Agostino and Pearson’s test for normality:

We will estimate whether the distributions are different. hourly entries is highly unlikely Gaussian so we will use nonparamertic Mann-Whitney U-Test. We test the null hypothesis that two populations are the same. As the reported p-value is for a one-sided hypothesis, to get the two-sided p-value we multiply the returned p-value by 2:

In [21]:
(U,p) = scipy.stats.mannwhitneyu(with_rain, without_rain, use_continuity=True)
print "U-statistic for Mann-Whitney U-Test is: %f" % U
#print "p-value for Mann-Whitney U-Test is: %f" % p
# As the function returns p = nan, we compute p from U:
m_u = len(with_rain)*len(without_rain)/2
sigma_u = np.sqrt(len(with_rain)*len(without_rain)*(len(with_rain)+len(without_rain)+1)/12)
z = (U - m_u)/sigma_u
p = 2*scipy.stats.norm.cdf(z)
print "p-value for Mann-Whitney U-Test is: %f" % p

U-statistic for Mann-Whitney U-Test is: 153635120.500000
p-value for Mann-Whitney U-Test is: 0.000005

As p-value is very small, we reject the null hypothesis. Therefore the alternative hypothesis that dictributions of ridership in the rain and no rain samples are different is statistically significant.

The mean for the rain data is bigger then the mean for the no rain data. We test the null hypothesis that the population means for rain samples and no rain samples are equal. As we have a plenty of samples, we can apply Welch's t-test. We use a one-tailed test to check the hypothesis that mean for samples with rain is larger then one without rain. We need to use p/2 value and check if t > 0.

As p-value is much less then any reasonable threshold the null hypothesis of equality of means is highly unlikely. Therefore, the alternative hypothesis that mean of the ridership in the rain is bigger then one without rain is statistically significant.

Section 2. Linear Regression¶

We split the data into training and testing sets. We randomly sample 70% of data as the training set. The rest 30% data are the testing set. We will build a linear regression model using Statsmodels OLS in the train set and estimate the model in the test set.

Section 4. Conclusion¶

4.1. From our analysis and interpretation of the data, we have got the following conclusions. On average, more people do ride the NYC subway when it is raining then it is not raining. But, in general, less people ride the NYC subway when it is raining then it is not raining, just because it is mostly clear in NYC. The ridership distributions are statistically different for raining and not raining cases. The mean of the ridership for raining cases is statistically bigger then the mean of the ridership for not raining cases.

4.2. We relied on statistical tests. In particular, we used nonparamertic Mann-Whitney U-Test for testing hypothesis that populations for raining and not raining cases are the same. Also we used Welch's one-tailed t-test to check the hypothesis that mean for samples with rain is equal to ones without rain. In both cases we regected the hypothesis. Therefore it is highly likely that distributions are different and the mean for samples with rain is bigger then for samples without rain.

Linear regression model supports our conclusion: all "rainy" regression coefficients are positive; the case 'conds_Rain' > 0 is statistically significant at 5% level.

The visualisation of mean ridership by hours also supports our conclusion that mean ridership is bigger for rainy cases then for non-rainy ones.

Section 5. Reflection¶

5.1. We wrangled NYC Subway data and Weather data before, so the dataset is clean. The dependent variable is marked as 'ENTRIESn_hourly' in spite of the data is sampled by 4 hours intervals.
